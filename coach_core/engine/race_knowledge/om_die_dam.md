# Om Die Dam Ultra Marathon — Race Intelligence

## Overview
- Distance: 50 km
- Route: Hartbeespoort Dam loop, North West Province
- Date: February (typically second or third Saturday)
- Cut-off: 8 hours
- Elevation gain: ~500 m (rolling hills around the dam)
- Character: "Around the Dam." One of SA's most popular ultras — a stepping stone to Two Oceans and Comrades. Excellent entry-level ultra for runners moving up from the marathon distance. Festive, well-organised, very popular.

## Course Profile

| Segment | Distance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Start — Hartbeespoort | 0–10 km | Rolling hills departing town. Legs fresh, don't go out too fast. |
| North side of dam | 10–22 km | Undulating along the north shore. Scenic dam views. |
| Schoemansville / Meerhof | 22–32 km | The hillier section. This is where the course tests you. |
| South side / Kosmos | 32–42 km | Rolling return. Beautiful scenery but fatigue sets in. |
| Return to start | 42–50 km | The last 8 km feels longer than it is. Manage this section. |

## Key Hills
- **The initial climb out of Hartbeespoort** (~5–8 km): Sets the tone. Control effort here.
- **Meerhof hills (~24–28 km):** The most demanding section. Consistent rolling climbs. Walk the steep ones.
- **Final rollers (42–50 km):** Small hills that feel enormous at 45 km. Stay patient.

## Pacing Strategy
1. **This is an ultra, not a long marathon.** Start 20–30 seconds/km slower than your marathon race pace. The dam loop has enough cumulative climbing that any fast start is punished.
2. **Run/walk from the start is not just acceptable — it's smart.** Adopting 8 minutes running / 1–2 minutes walking from early on preserves legs for the back half.
3. **The halfway checkpoint is a checkpoint, not a pitstop.** Keep moving. Time spent sitting down is very hard to recover.
4. **The last 10 km is slow for everyone.** Bank nothing — spend energy steadily and uniformly.

## Nutrition Strategy
- Tables approximately every 3 km from ~10 km.
- Water, sports drink, Coke (from ~30 km), bananas, oranges, boiled potatoes with salt.
- Gels: every 40 min for first half, every 30 min in the second half.
- **Salt is critical.** February is hot. Heat + effort = sodium loss. Take salt sachets or electrolyte tablets hourly.
- **Start eating before you're hungry.** At 50 km, hunger arrives late — by then you're bonking.

## Weather
- February in North West: very hot and humid for South African conditions. 25–35°C on course during the race. Start (6:00 AM) is cooler — 18–24°C — but heats up fast.
- **This is typically the hottest of the major SA ultras.** Heat management is critical.
- Heat strategy: slow your target pace by 15–20 sec/km for every 5°C above 20°C.
- Drink more than you think you need. Sweat loss in Highveld summer heat is enormous.
- Occasional afternoon thunderstorms — unlikely at 6 AM start but the humidity builds through the morning.

## Gear & Clothing
- Lightweight, breathable kit. This is a hot-weather race.
- Hat and sunscreen are not optional.
- Anti-chafe everything — 50 km in heat and sweat.
- Road shoes throughout. No trail sections.

## Cut-Off Guidance
- 8-hour cut-off = 9:36/km average pace including all stops.
- Comfortably in? Build in a 30-minute buffer: aim to finish in 7:30.
- Conservative approach: aim for 5:00–6:30 depending on your fitness level.

## Common Mistakes
- Going out at marathon pace — this is an ultra, not a long marathon
- Underestimating the heat — February is genuinely hot
- Neglecting salt (not just fluids) in hot conditions
- Not walking early enough — pride costs legs

## Using Om Die Dam as a Stepping Stone
- Om Die Dam is an excellent qualifier and training run for Two Oceans (56 km) and Comrades (90 km).
- Running it 6–8 weeks before Two Oceans tells you a great deal about your ultra readiness.
- Target: finish comfortably with energy in reserve, not a death march.
